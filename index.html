About Us

Our mission is to empower, celebrate, and elevate women in technology by providing a dynamic platform for news, historical insights, and growth opportunities. We are dedicated to telling the untold stories of trailblazers, spotlighting current innovators, and connecting future leaders with the resources, community, and inspiration they need to thrive. Through education, advocacy, and representation, we aim to shape a more inclusive and equitable tech industry for all.

Solutions

  1. Lack of Representation & Visibility Struggles: Few female role models in leadership positions. Limited media coverage of women’s contributions in tech. Solutions/Resources: Communities & Networks: Women Who Code AnitaB.org TechLadies Events & Conferences: Grace Hopper Celebration Women in Tech Global Conference
  2. Gender Bias & Workplace Discrimination Struggles: Implicit bias in hiring and promotions. Unequal pay and lack of recognition. Solutions/Resources: Bias & Inclusion Training for teams and leadership. Salary Transparency Tools: Glassdoor Payscale Supportive HR Practices and allyship programs in tech companies.
  3. Limited Mentorship & Career Growth Struggles: Few accessible mentors or sponsors. Lack of clear career pathways. Solutions/Resources: Mentorship Platforms: Built By Girls MentorCruise Career Development Programs: Google's Women Techmakers program LinkedIn Learning & Coursera tech tracks
  4. Work-Life Balance & Retention Struggles: Difficulty balancing career growth with personal/family responsibilities. High attrition rates among mid-career women in tech. Solutions/Resources: Flexible Work Policies (remote options, parental leave). Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) for women in tech. Mental Health Support & work-life integration coaching.
  5. Access to Funding & Entrepreneurship Support Struggles: Underfunded women-led startups. Limited VC access for female founders. Solutions/Resources: Women-Focused VC Firms & Accelerators: Female Founders Fund All Raise Backstage Capital Pitch Competitions & Grants: Cartier Women’s Initiative She Loves Tech competition
  6. Organizations that support women in tech:
    • Black Girls Code

    Black Girls Code is a nonprofit founded in 2011 to empower Black girls and girls of color through coding and STEM education. It offers programs like workshops, camps, and clubs to teach skills in programming, robotics, and tech development. The organization aims to train one million girls by 2040, helping them become future leaders in the tech industry.

    • Girls Who Code

    Girls Who Code is a nonprofit organization dedicated to closing the gender gap in technology by equipping young women with computer science skills. Through clubs, summer programs, and college initiatives, it empowers girls to pursue careers in tech. The organization fosters a supportive community that celebrates sisterhood, bravery, and resilience.

    • NCWIT

    The National Center for Women & Information Technology (NCWIT) is a nonprofit that works to increase the participation of women and girls in computing. It supports schools, businesses, and organizations with programs and resources to promote inclusion in tech. NCWIT aims to create lasting change by helping more women enter and thrive in the tech industry.

Gender Pay Gap

  1. What Is the Gender Pay Gap? It’s not just about men and women doing different jobs. It often exists even when they do the same job with the same qualifications. It’s usually expressed as a percentage: Example: If women earn 90 cents for every dollar a man earns, the pay gap is 10%.

Notable Women In Tech

  • Grace Hopper

    Grace Brewster Hopper (née Murray; December 9, 1906 – January 1, 1992) was an American computer scientist, mathematician, and United States Navy rear admiral. [1] She was a pioneer of computer programming. Hopper was the first to devise the theory of machine-independent programming languages, and used this theory to develop the FLOW-MATIC programming language and COBOL, an early high-level programming language still in use today. She was also one of the first programmers on the Harvard Mark I computer. She is credited with writing the first computer manual, "A Manual of Operation for the Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator."

  • Ada LoveLace

    Ada Lovelace, was an English mathematician and writer chiefly known for her work on Charles Babbage's proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine. She was the first to recognise that the machine had applications beyond pure calculation.

  • Annie Easley

    Annie Easley (April 23, 1933 – June 25, 2011) was an African American computer scientist and mathematician who made critical contributions to NASA's rocket systems and energy technologies.
